Layer I — Analytical Engine
Step 1 of 5
Sector Selection Logic
Which industries does China choose to fund?
Models how a GGF-administering state determines which sectors warrant priority investment. Seven variables are weighted via a convex combination. Rankings are derived, not asserted — adjust the weight sliders to observe how the priority ordering shifts under different policy mandates (security-first vs. efficiency-first vs. growth-driven).
China's government can't fund every industry — so it picks. This page lets you see how that picking decision works, and lets you change the rules yourself. Move the sliders on the left to change what the government cares about most. The ranking list on the right updates automatically.
🟦 Plain English — What's happening on this page
Think of this like a scoring quiz for industries. Each industry (semiconductors, AI, electric cars, etc.) gets a score from 0–10 on 7 questions — things like "how much does China rely on imports for this?" or "is this important for national defence?". You decide how important each question is by moving the sliders. The industry with the highest total score gets ranked #1 and would receive the most political attention and funding priority. Try clicking "Security-First" or "Market-Efficiency" below the sliders to see how the rankings completely change based on what the government cares about.

The default weights (SV=7, TF=6, NS=8, SP=5, TH=4, RB=3, PM=9) are calibrated from the revealed preference ordering in PRC industrial policy documents 2015–2023. They are not normative prescriptions — they represent what decision-making behaviour implies, not what it should be.

Layer II — Capital Engine
Step 2 of 5
Capital Determination Model
How much money does each industry actually get?
Derives fund sizes from structural variables — not arbitrary decree. Capital intensity, risk profile, innovation cycle length, and leverage capacity interact under a fiscal constraint to produce sector-level budgets. The government's role is modelled as seed capital that signals and unlocks private co-investment. The leverage multiplier is endogenous to risk-adjusted returns and crowding-in potential.
Once an industry is chosen, the next question is: how many billions of yuan does it actually receive? The answer depends on how expensive the industry is to build, how risky it is, and whether private businesses will join in with their own money. Use the sliders to change China's budget or risk appetite and watch the numbers shift.
🟦 Plain English — What's happening on this page
Imagine China has a big pot of money (set with the first slider). It splits this across industries based on: how much it costs to build that industry, how risky it is, and how much extra money private companies will add on top. The government's money is the seed. The private money that follows is the harvest. When private money doesn't follow (e.g. too risky), you get the 🚩 "sleeping capital" problem — government money is committed but sits idle.

⚑ Structural Contradiction — Positive LogicKey paradox — the harder the industry, the more it gets promised but never delivered
Positive High-risk, long-cycle sectors (Semiconductors, Quantum Computing) attract the largest government commitments precisely because private capital will not bear the risk. Yet the leverage multiplier for these sectors is the lowest, meaning total deployed capital falls far short of committed figures. This gap between commitment and deployment is the central inefficiency of the GGF mechanism — politically visible but financially inert. The industries that need the most help (like chips and quantum computing) get the biggest government promises — but ironically receive the least actual money in practice. Why? Because private companies won't co-invest in industries that are too risky or too experimental. So the government's committed money just sits there, unspent. It looks impressive on paper but doesn't actually build anything.
Layer III — Optimisation Engine
Step 3 of 5
Allocation Weighting Mechanism
How is the total money split across industries?
Allocation percentages emerge from a multi-objective optimisation balancing security, growth, innovation, and political incentives — subject to institutional constraints. This is explicitly not a Pareto-efficient allocation. Institutional distortions (risk aversion, bandwagon effects, principal-agent misalignment) create systematic deviations from any theoretically optimal frontier. The three scenarios make visible what changes and what remains structurally fixed.
This page shows the percentage slice each industry gets out of the total pot. Switch between three policy modes using the buttons below. The key finding: the actual slice China's industries receive is never quite optimal — political pressures, herd behaviour, and bureaucratic caution all push the allocation away from what the math says would be best.
🟦 Plain English — What's happening on this page
Even with a priority list and a budget, there's still a question: what percentage of the total money should each industry get? A government focused on national security will give more to chips and aerospace. One focused on growth will give more to AI and electric vehicles. The three buttons let you switch between these mindsets. The distortion table shows where China's real-world behaviour differs from what any of these approaches would recommend — and why.

Layer IV — Friction Analysis
Step 4 of 5
Institutional Frictions & Misallocation
What goes wrong with the money system?
The GGF mechanism generates systematic efficiency losses that are structurally endogenous — not accidents or failures of design, but predictable outcomes of the principal-agent architecture. Four friction classes are modelled: sleeping capital (committed but undeployed), bandwagon overconcentration, principal-agent misalignment between GP incentives and state objectives, and bureaucratic risk aversion producing late-stage investment bias.
Even with a good plan and enough money, four structural problems reliably make the GGF system less effective than it should be. This page shows exactly how much efficiency is lost to each problem, and which industries suffer most. None of these are accidents — they're predictable consequences of how the system is designed.
🟦 Plain English — The four things that go wrong
1. Sleeping capital: Money promised but never spent — like a cheque that never gets cashed. 2. Herd behaviour (bandwagon): Everyone piles into the same politically popular industries (currently AI), so those get too much and others too little. 3. Manager vs. government conflict: The fund managers (who invest the money day-to-day) care about their own bonuses and reputation, not the government's strategic goals. 4. Fear of failure: Government officials are punished for backing a risky project that fails, but not for ignoring a great opportunity — so they always choose the safe, boring option.

⚑ Sleeping Capital Paradox
Positive Approximately 40% of committed GGF capital remains undeployed across the system (Zero2IPO, 2023). This is not primarily a funding shortage problem — it is an absorption capacity problem. Sectors with the highest strategic priority (Semiconductors) also have the highest sleeping capital rates because GP-level fund managers cannot identify sufficient quality deal flow, particularly in early-stage fabless design and EDA tooling.
⚑ Principal-Agent Misalignment
Positive GPs managing GGFs are evaluated on financial returns and AUM growth, not on strategic objective achievement. This creates incentives to (a) concentrate in later-stage, lower-risk deals that produce marketable track records; (b) over-allocate to visible, politically salient sectors regardless of expected returns; and (c) underreport sleeping capital to avoid mandated return-of-capital penalties.
Layer V — Shock Simulation
Step 5 of 5
External Shock Reconfiguration
What happens when a crisis hits?
Models how the GGF allocation vector reconfigurates under exogenous shocks. Shocks operate by modifying underlying variable scores (SV, NS, TF, etc.) rather than directly adjusting allocations — the logic is that shocks change the information set available to decision-makers, which then flows through the scoring and capital determination functions to produce a new equilibrium allocation.
The world doesn't always cooperate with China's investment plans. Pick a crisis scenario from the dropdown — like US chip sanctions, or a global pandemic — and the model recalculates everything: which industries suddenly become more urgent, which get deprioritised, and what happens to the money flows. Use the second dropdown to compare two crises side by side.

Mathematical Specification
Layer I — Sector Priority Score
P(s) = Σᵢ [wᵢ × Vᵢ(s)] / Σᵢ wᵢ
i ∈ {SV, TF, NS, SP, TH, RB, PM}
Vᵢ ∈ [0,10], wᵢ ∈ [1,10], s = sector
Layer II — Capital Allocation
A(s) = B × [P(s)·CI(s)^0.5] / Σₛ[…]
G(s) = A(s) / (1 + λ(s) · ρ)
L(s) = A(s) · λ(s) · ρ
B=budget, λ=leverage ratio, ρ=risk tolerance
Layer III — Multi-Objective Optimisation
max Σₛ wₛ[α·Sec + β·Grw + γ·Inn + δ·Pol]
s.t. Σₛ wₛ = 1, wₛ ≥ 0
Δ(s) = θ·Band(s) − ε·RA(s) − η·PA(s)
w_actual(s) = w*(s) + Δ(s)
Layer I — Sector Priority Score
P(s) = [w₁·SV(s) + w₂·TF(s) + w₃·NS(s) + w₄·SP(s) + w₅·TH(s) + w₆·RB(s) + w₇·PM(s)] / Σᵢwᵢ

All Vᵢ(s) ∈ [0,10] are empirically calibrated scores (see data table below). Default weights: w = [7,6,8,5,4,3,9] reflecting the revealed preference ordering in PRC industrial policy documents, where political mandate alignment (PM) and national security (NS) dominate observed sector selection over 2015–2023.

Variable definitions:
SV = Strategic Vulnerability (import dependence + bottleneck severity)
TF = Technology Frontier Gap (distance from global frontier; higher = more urgent)
NS = National Security Relevance (dual-use potential + supply chain criticality)
SP = Spillover Effects (industrial commons + adjacent sector impact)
TH = Time Horizon Compatibility (short cycle = higher score; penalises 10yr+ cycles)
RB = Regional Base Capacity (existing industrial infrastructure)
PM = Political Mandate Alignment (match to 5-Year Plan & Xi-era priority signals)
NoteThe convex combination with adjustable weights models how different administrative configurations (State Council vs. provincial SASAC vs. military-civil fusion committees) produce different effective weightings on the same underlying variables. The model's preset profiles approximate observed variation across these institutions.
Layer II — Capital Determination
Base share: φ(s) = P(s)·CI(s)^0.5 / Σₛ[P(s)·CI(s)^0.5]
Total committed: A(s) = B · φ(s)
Government seed: G(s) = A(s) / (1 + λ(s)·ρ)
Expected private: L(s) = A(s) · λ(s)·ρ / (1 + λ(s)·ρ)
Sleeping capital: SC(s) = G(s) · [0.25 + 0.15·(Risk(s)/10) + 0.10·(1−RB(s)/10)]

CI(s)^0.5 is the capital intensity adjustment: using the square root dampens the mechanical advantage of high-capex sectors, reflecting that GGFs do not linearly scale government commitment with capital intensity (the private sector is expected to shoulder high capex in mature sectors). λ(s) is the sector-specific historical leverage ratio from empirical data; ρ ∈ [0.1,1.0] is the risk tolerance scalar. The sleeping capital formula compounds three risk factors: baseline bureaucratic inertia (25%), early-stage risk premium, and weak local industrial base.
Layer III — Allocation Distortion
Objective functions (normalised to [0,1]):
Sec(s) = 0.5·SV(s)/10 + 0.3·NS(s)/10 + 0.2·(10−TH_inv(s))/10
Grw(s) = 0.4·SP(s)/10 + 0.3·CI_inv(s)/10 + 0.3·(1/cycle(s))·10
Inn(s) = 0.6·TF(s)/10 + 0.4·risk(s)/10
Pol(s) = PM(s)/10

Optimal: w*(s) = [α·Sec(s) + β·Grw(s) + γ·Inn(s) + δ·Pol(s)] / Σₛ[...]
Distortion: Δ(s) = θ·Band(s) − ε·RA(s) − η·PA(s)
Actual: w(s) = w*(s) + Δ(s), then renormalised to Σw=1

θ=0.08 (bandwagon coefficient, calibrated to Zero2IPO sector concentration data); ε=0.06 (risk aversion penalty for sectors with risk score >7); η=0.04 (GP underperformance penalty from Lu et al. 2023). Band(s)=1 if PM>8.5, else 0; RA(s) = max(0, risk(s)−7)/3; PA(s) = max(0, cycle(s)−6)/9.
